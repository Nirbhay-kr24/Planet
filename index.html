Astronomy Picture of the Day

SOLAR SYSTEM

MERCURY

92.00 million km

Moons: 0

Mercury is the closest planet to the Sun, with a diameter of 4,880 km. It has no atmosphere, causing extreme temperature variations, reaching 430°C during the day and -180°C at night. Mercury’s orbit takes 88 Earth days, and it rotates very slowly, completing one spin in 59 days. The planet's surface is heavily cratered, similar to Earth's Moon. Mercury has no moons and a very weak magnetic field. Its surface experiences intense solar radiation. Despite being closest to the Sun, it is not the hottest planet.

VENUS

41.40 million km

Moons: 0

Venus, with a diameter of 12,104 km, is Earth’s “sister planet” because of its similar size. Venus has a thick, toxic atmosphere mainly of carbon dioxide, causing an extreme greenhouse effect that makes it the hottest planet in the Solar System, with temperatures reaching 465°C. The planet rotates slowly in the opposite direction to most planets, taking 243 Earth days to complete one rotation. Venus has no moons. Its surface is covered by volcanic plains and mountain ranges. Despite its beauty, Venus’s surface is uninhabitable.

EARTH

diameter: 12,742 km

Moons: 1

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only astronomical object known to harbor life. It is unique for supporting life, with diverse environments and only planet as of now with liquid water covering 71% of its surface. Earth's atmosphere, composed mainly of nitrogen and oxygen, supports life. It has one moon, which stabilizes its axial tilt. Earth's day lasts 24 hours, and its year is 365 days. Earth experiences seasons due to its axial tilt, making it suitable for life. Earth is the only known planet to support complex life forms.

MARS

78.34 million km

Moons: 2

Mars, often called the "Red Planet," has a diameter of 6,779 km. The reddish hue comes from iron oxide, or rust, on its surface. Mars has a thin atmosphere mostly made of carbon dioxide. Temperatures average -60°C, but they can reach -125°C at the poles. Mars has two small moons, Phobos and Deimos. The planet’s surface features include the largest volcano in the Solar System, Olympus Mons, and deep valleys, including Valles Marineris. Mars shows signs of ancient water, with riverbeds and polar ice caps.

JUPITER

628.73 million km

Moons: 95

Jupiter is the largest planet in the Solar System, with a diameter of 139,820 km. It is a gas giant composed mostly of hydrogen and helium. Jupiter has a Great Red Spot, a massive storm that has lasted for centuries. Its atmosphere features high-speed winds and massive cloud bands. Jupiter has 95 known moons, including four large Galilean moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. The planet’s magnetic field is the strongest of any planet, and it has a rapid rotation, completing one day in just 10 hours.

SATURN

1,275.50 million km

Moons: 146

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun and the second largest in the Solar System, after Jupiter. It has a diameter of 116,460 km. It is a gas giant, primarily composed of hydrogen and helium, and is famous for its beautiful ring system. The rings are made of ice and rock particles. Saturn has at least 146 moons, with Titan being the largest. Titan is unique for having a thick atmosphere and lakes of liquid methane. Saturn’s winds can reach speeds of 1,800 km/h. The planet’s rotation takes just 10.7 hours, but its orbit around the Sun takes 29.5 Earth years.

URANUS

2,723.95 million km

Moons: 27

Uranus is an ice giant with a diameter of 50,724 km. It is composed mainly of hydrogen, helium, and water, along with methane, which gives it a pale blue color. Uranus is unique for its extreme axial tilt of 98 degrees, making it rotate on its side. This results in extreme seasonal variations. The planet has 27 known moons, including Miranda and Titania. Uranus has a faint ring system and experiences high-speed winds. Its average surface temperature is -224°C, making it one of the coldest planets in the Solar System.

NEPTUNE

4,353.00 million km

Moons: 14

Neptune is the eighth planet from the Sun, with a diameter of 49,244 km. It is an ice giant composed mostly of hydrogen, helium, and methane. Neptune has a deep blue color due to methane in its atmosphere. It experiences the fastest winds in the Solar System, reaching 2,100 km/h. Neptune has 14 known moons. Triton has retrograde motion, meaning it orbits Neptune in the opposite direction of the planet’s rotation. Neptune's Great Dark Spot is a massive storm similar to Jupiter’s Red Spot.

PLUTO

5,906.38 million km

Moons: 5

Pluto is a dwarf planet with a diameter of 2,377 km. It orbits in the Kuiper Belt beyond Neptune and has a highly elliptical orbit. Pluto’s surface is a mix of rock and ice, and its thin atmosphere consists mainly of nitrogen, with small amounts of methane and carbon monoxide. Pluto has five known moons, Styx, Nix, Kerberos, Hydra, and Charon being the largest. The planet experiences extremely cold temperatures, averaging around -225°C. Pluto was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006 by the International Astronomical Union.

SUN

150.00 million km

The Sun is a star at the center of the Solar System, providing essential light and heat to Earth. Composed mostly of hydrogen and helium, it undergoes nuclear fusion in its core. The Sun has a diameter of 1.39 million km and is 109 times larger than Earth. It’s approximately 4.6 billion years old. The Sun’s gravity keeps the planets in orbit, while its solar wind influences space weather. It will continue shining for about 5 billion more years, eventually expanding into a red giant.From Earth, it is 1 astronomical unit (1.496×108 km) or 8 light-minutes away.

ISRO Mission Chandrayaan

The Chandrayaan programme (Sanskrit: Candra 'Moon', Yāna 'Craft, Vehicle',) also known as the Indian Lunar Exploration Programme is an ongoing series of outer space missions by the Indian Space Research Organization (ISRO) for the exploration of the Moon. The program incorporates a lunar orbiter, an impactor, a soft lander and a rover spacecraft. There have been three missions so far with a total of two orbiters, landers and rovers each. While the two orbiters were successful, the first lander and rover which were part of the Chandrayaan-2 mission, crashed on the surface. The second lander and rover mission Chandrayaan-3 successfully landed on the Moon on 23 August 2023, making India the first nation to successfully land a spacecraft in the lunar south pole region, and the fourth country to soft land on the Moon after the Soviet Union, the United States and China.
